((77) Our companions and others said that making a picture of an animal is strictly forbidden and is one of the major sins. Whether it is made for something that is used in a dishonorable manner or for something else, it is forbidden in all cases because it is an imitation of God’s creation. Whether it is on a garment, a rug, a dinar, a dirham, a coin, a vessel, or a wall. As for that which does not have a picture of an animal, such as a tree and the like, it is not forbidden. Whether in all of this it is something that has a shadow or something that does not have a shadow, and the same meaning was said by a group of scholars, Malik, al-Thawri, Abu Hanifa, and others (“Umdat al-Qari Sharh Sahih al-Bukhari,” vol. 22, p. 70, publisher: Dar Ihya’ al-Turath al-Arabi, Beirut).

- Our companions and other scholars said: Drawing an image of an animal is strictly forbidden, and it is one of the major sins, because it is subject to this severe warning mentioned in the hadiths, and whether it is made in a way that is dishonored or otherwise, its making is forbidden in all cases, because it is an imitation of the creation of Allah Almighty, and whether it is on a garment, a carpet, a dirham, a dinar, a penny, a vessel, a wall, or anything else. As for drawing an image of trees, camel saddles, and other things that do not have an image of an animal, it is not forbidden. This is the ruling on the image itself. As for making an image that has an image of an animal, if it is hung on a wall, a garment that is worn, a turban, or something similar that is not considered dishonored, then it is forbidden. If it is on a carpet that is stepped on, a pillow, a cushion, or something similar that is dishonored, then it is not forbidden. But does it prevent the angels of mercy from entering that house? There is a discussion about this that we will mention soon, God willing, and there is no difference in all of this between what has a shadow and what does not. This is a summary of our doctrine on the issue, and the majority of scholars from the Companions, the Followers, and those who came after them said the same. It is the doctrine of al-Thawri, Malik, Abu Hanifah, and others. Some of the Salaf said: What is forbidden is only what has a shadow, and there is nothing wrong with pictures that do not have a shadow. This is a false doctrine; for the curtain in which the Prophet (peace and blessings of Allaah be upon him) denounced the image, no one doubts that it is reprehensible, and its image does not have a shadow, along with the rest of the general hadeeths regarding every image. Al-Zuhri said: The prohibition of a picture is general, as is the use of what it is in, and entering a house in which it is, whether it is a number on a garment or not, and whether it is on a wall, a garment, or a carpet that is used or not used, based on the apparent meaning of the hadeeths, especially the hadeeth of “the carpet” mentioned by Muslim. This is a strong doctrine. Others said: What is permissible is what is a number on a garment, whether it is dishonored or not, and whether it is hung on a wall or not. They disliked what has a shadow, or is pictured on walls and the like, whether it is a number or something else. They cited as evidence his saying in some of the hadiths of the chapter: (“Except what is a number on a garment”). This is the school of thought of Al-Qasim bin Muhammad. They agreed unanimously to prohibit what has a shadow, and to require changing it. The judge said: Except what was mentioned about playing with dolls for young girls, and the permission for that, but Malik disliked a man buying that for his daughter. Some of them claimed that the permissibility of playing with dolls for them was abrogated by these hadiths, and God knows best. (“Al-Minhaj” An-Nawawi’s explanation of Sahih Muslim, Vol. 7, p. 205, Publisher: Dar Ihya’ Al-Turath Al-Arabi, Beirut).
